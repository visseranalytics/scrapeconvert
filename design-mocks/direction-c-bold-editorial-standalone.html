Where your data goes

Two jobs, two honest answers

Plenty of tools claim everything stays on your device. That is not true once you scrape, so here is the precise version for each half of the app.

Converting is fully local

When you convert images, the work runs inside your browser with WebAssembly. Those image bytes never leave your machine. There is no upload, no server step, and nothing to delete afterward.

Scraping passes through a proxy

Browsers will not let a page read images from another domain, so the URL you scrape and the bytes it fetches travel through our Cloudflare Worker. That is the only reason it exists: to clear the CORS wall. We do not store what passes through it.

The proxy is rate-limited and protected by a one-time bot check per session. It refuses private and internal addresses, and it skips paid stock-photo sites. Analytics, if any, are privacy-friendly counts. There is no ad tracking anywhere in the app.

FAQ

Questions, answered plainly

Is it free?

Yes. ScrapeConvert is free and open source under the MIT license. There is no paid tier and no upsell. If it saves you time, you can leave a tip on buy-me-a-coffee, but nothing is gated behind it.

Do you store my images?

Conversion is local, so the files you convert never leave your browser. Scraping is different: the page and image fetches travel through our proxy to get past CORS. We do not store anything that passes through the proxy, but it is honest to say those bytes do transit a server on the way to you.

Why does scraping need a proxy?

Browser security stops one site from reading the contents of another. That rule, called CORS, blocks a page in your tab from fetching images off a different domain. The proxy makes the request on your behalf so the images can come back. It does the one thing the browser will not let the page do directly.

Is the scraping respectful?

It is built to be. The proxy only pulls public images, refuses private and internal addresses, and skips paid stock-photo sites. It is rate-limited and gated by a one-time bot check per session, so it cannot be turned into a high-volume scraping engine. Use it to collect images you have a right to use.

Can I self-host it?

Yes. The whole thing runs on Cloudflare, including the Worker that handles scraping. Clone the repo, deploy it to your own account, and you control the proxy and its limits. Everything you need is in the GitHub readme.

Is there a browser extension?

Yes, there is a free Chrome extension. It scrapes images from whatever tab you have open, which is handy for pages behind a login where pasting a URL would not work. It hands the results to the same converter.
